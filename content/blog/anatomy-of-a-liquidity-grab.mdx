# The myth: stops are random.

When a retail trader gets stopped out, the story they tell themselves is
some version of "the market is unpredictable." It feels random because
the stop hit was uncomfortable. Discomfort and randomness are not the
same thing.

Stops are not random. They are inventory. To a venue with size, a
cluster of stops above an obvious high is a known location with known
volume — and the cheapest way to acquire that inventory is to make
price touch it.

This is the four-step structure of every textbook liquidity raid. Once
you can name the steps, you stop trading inside them.

## Step 1 — Range build

Price compresses. Two-, three-, sometimes five-day balance. The range
high gets retested twice, the range low gets retested twice, and the
trader watching it forms an opinion about which side will break.

That opinion is the product. The range exists to manufacture conviction
on one side, so that conviction can be harvested on the other.

## Step 2 — Sweep

Price breaks the obvious side first — the side that "everyone is
watching." Stops sitting just above the high get filled. New breakout
longs get filled too. Both groups now have positions in the same
direction at the same price.

This is not a breakout. This is **inventory acquisition** disguised as
a breakout. Volume usually spikes here. The candle that does the sweep
is often a wide-range close that looks aggressive — that's the bait.

## Step 3 — Reclaim

Price comes back inside the range. Fast. Often within one or two bars.
The breakout longs and the stop-fills from step 2 are now underwater.

The structural marker for this is a **break of structure (BOS)** in the
opposite direction — usually a clean displacement back through the
range mid. If you missed step 2, this is your last warning.

## Step 4 — Drive

Price drives to the *opposite* side of the range. The longs from step
2 unwind. The shorts that were waiting on the range low get filled.
The move is one-sided because the supply is one-sided — every long
caught at the high is a forced seller.

The textbook target is the equal-low or equal-high on the opposite
side of the original range. That's where the next pool of stops sits.
The cycle repeats.

## Why your autopsy keeps showing the same flag

If your last three losing trades all flag as "liquidity sweep into
reversal," it isn't a coincidence. You're entering inside step 2 every
time. The conviction the range manufactured was *for you*.

Three rules that move the needle once you can identify the structure:

1. **Don't trade the breakout.** The first break of an obvious level
   is the trap. The second break — *after* a sweep and reclaim — is
   the move.
2. **Mark the equal highs/lows.** Liquidity always pools at obvious
   prior turns. If you can't identify where the next pool of stops
   sits, you cannot tell which side is being hunted.
3. **The candle that sweeps is the candle to fade — not the one to
   join.** Wide-range break candles into a known liquidity zone are
   the most expensive entries on the chart.
