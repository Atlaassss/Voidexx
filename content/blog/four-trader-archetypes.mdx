# The taxonomy.

The Voidexx engine has run a lot of autopsies. After a few thousand,
the verdicts cluster. The market does not have infinite ways to take
you out — it has a small number of *shapes*, repeated in different
clothes. Recognise the shape and you recognise the future.

These are the four archetypes. Every closed trade you've ever placed
fits one of them.

## 1. The Clean OB Long

**Profile:** Patient entry. Refined order block. Confluence of higher
timeframe bias and a fresh imbalance below. Stop tucked below the
zone, target a previous structural high. R:R north of 3.

**Structural signature:** BOS up on a higher timeframe → pullback into
an OB → refined entry on the lower timeframe with a smaller OB or FVG
at the same price.

**Why it works:** The trader did the homework. They waited. The
market gave them a clean retracement into a level with multiple
reasons to hold.

**The catch:** This is the rarest archetype. Most traders see *one*
of these per ten attempts. The other nine are the next three
archetypes — and traders who haven't done the post-mortem can't tell
the difference in the moment.

> If your win-rate is 35% and your average winner is a 3R clean OB
> long, you are a profitable trader. Most "losing" traders are
> actually winning traders who don't see their own pattern.

## 2. The Liquidity Trap Short

**Profile:** Sold into a sweep of the range high because "it looked
like a fakeout." Got the direction right. Stop above the wick. The
wick extended. Stop hit. Price reversed within five candles.

**Structural signature:** Sweep of equal-highs → first reaction
candle is small → stop is placed inside the sweep range, not above
it.

**The verdict the engine writes:** *"Right call, wrong stop."* The
read on direction was correct. The stop placement assumed the sweep
would be precise. Sweeps are rarely precise. The trader who
understands liquidity hunts places the stop **above the entire wick
range** of comparable historical sweeps on the same instrument, not
above the immediate wick.

**Fix:** Pull a six-month sample of sweeps on the asset you trade.
Measure the average wick extension past the equal-high in points or
ticks. That number is your minimum buffer. Anything tighter is an
invitation.

## 3. The FOMO Chase

**Profile:** Saw a candle. Felt the move. Entered late. Got immediate
heat. Closed at break-even or down. Re-entered when it kept moving.
Got stopped on the pullback.

**Structural signature:** Entry occurs *after* a major impulse move
has already extended past two prior structural levels. There is no
pullback into anything. The trader is paying retail for a position
that smart money is already distributing.

**The psychological tell:** The autopsy almost always finds two or
more entries within 10 minutes. The second entry is a tell — the
first one was too cautious to size up; the second one was emotional
revenge for missing the move.

**Fix:** A hard rule that no entry happens within X minutes of the
candle close that broke structure. The exact X is per-asset, but the
floor is "long enough that the impulse candle has fully closed and
the next bar has confirmed direction."

## 4. The Patient Sniper

**Profile:** Top of the leaderboard. Three trades a week. 2.5R
average. Win-rate is 50% — they are not magicians, they are
disciplined.

**Structural signature:** Every entry has the same checklist:
higher-timeframe trend agreement, refined OB or FVG, sweep + reclaim
*before* entry, R:R minimum 2.0, position size locked at 0.5% of
account.

**Why this is in the taxonomy:** Because it's the archetype most
traders *want* to be but don't have the patience for. Watching the
patient sniper trade is boring. Most days they don't trade. The
edge is in the absence of trades, not the presence of them.

> Discipline is the strategy. The setup is just the trigger.

## So what do you do with this?

When the autopsy comes back and assigns your trade to one of these
four buckets, the question is not "was that trade good or bad."
The question is **which bucket do my losses cluster in.**

Pull your last 30 closed trades. Run them through the engine. The
distribution will not be even. One bucket will dominate — that's the
shape of your bias. Fix that bucket and the win rate moves before
you change anything else.

Most "I need a new strategy" actually means "I need to stop trading
archetype 3."
